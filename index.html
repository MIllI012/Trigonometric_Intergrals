Trigonometric Integrals

In this section we use trigonometric identities to integrate certain combinations of trigonometric functions

Integrals of Powers of Sine and Cosine
We begin by considering integrals in which the integrand is a power of sine, a power of cosine, or a product of these.

EXAMPLE 1 Evaluate $\int cos^{3}\,{x}\,dx$

SOLUTION Simply substituting $u − cos\,{x}$ isn’t helpful, since then $du − 2sin\,{x}\,dx$. In order to integrate powers of cosine, we would need an extra sin x factor. Similarly, a power of sine would require an extra $cos\,{x}$ factor. Thus here we can separate one cosine factor and convert the remaining $cos^{2}\,{x}$ factor to an expression involving sine using the identity $sin^{2}\,{x} + cos^{2}\,{x} = 1$

$cos^{3}\,{x} = cos^{2}\,{x} \cdot cos\,{x} = (1 - sin^{2}\,{x}) \cdot cos\,{x}$

We can then evaluate the integral by substituting $u − sin\,{x}$, so $du − cos\,{x}\,dx$ and

$\int\,cos^{3}\,{x} = \int\,cos^{2}\,{x} \cdot cos\,{x} = \int\,(1 - sin^{2}\,{x}) \cdot cos\,{x}$
$\qquad=\int\,(1-u^{2})\,du = u - \tfrac {1} {3}u^{3} + C$
$\qquad=sin\,{x} - \tfrac {1} {3} sin^{3}\,{x} + C$

In general, we try to write an integrand involving powers of sine and cosine in a form where we have only one sine factor (and the remainder of the expression in terms of cosine) or only one cosine factor (and the remainder of the expression in terms of sine). The identity $sin^{2}\,{x} + cos^{2}\,{x} = 1$ enables us to convert back and forth between even powers of sine and cosine.

EXAMPLE 2 Find $\int\,sin^{5}\,{x}\,cos^{2}\,{x}\,dx$

SOLUTIONWe could convert $cos^{2} {x}$ to $1 - 2 sin^{2}\,{x}$, but we would be left with an expression in terms of $sin\,{x}$ with no extra $cos {x}$ factor. Instead, we separate a single sine factor and rewrite the remaining $sin^{4}\,{x}$ factor in terms of $cos\,{x}$:

$sin^{5}\,{x} \cdot cos^{2}\,{x}=(sin^{2}\,{x})^{2} \cdot cos^{2}\,{x}sin\,{x} = (1-cos^{2}\,{x})^{2} \cdot cos^{2}\,{x} \cdot sin\,{x}$

Substituting $u=cos\,{x}$, we have $du = -sin\,{x}\;dx$ and so

$\int \,sin^{5}\,{x}\cdot cos^{2}\,{x}\,dx\,=\,\int (sin^{2}\,{x})^{2} \cdot cos^{2}\,{x} \cdot sin\,{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\int\,(1-cos^{2}\,{x})^{2}\cdot cos^{2}\,{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\int\,(1-u^{2})^{2}u^{2}(-du)\;=\;-\int(u^{2}-2u^{4}+u^{6})\;du$
$\qquad=(\tfrac{u^{3}}{3}-2\tfrac{u^{5}}{5}+\tfrac{u^{7}}{7}\,+\,C)$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{3}cos^{3}\,{x}+\tfrac{2}{5}cos^{5}\,{x}-\tfrac{1}{7}cos^{7}\,{x}\,+\,C$

In the preceding examples, an odd power of sine or cosine enabled us to separate a single factor and convert the remaining even power. If the integrand contains even powers of both sine and cosine, this strategy fails. In this case, we can take advantage of the following half-angle identities (see Equations 18b and 18a in Appendix D):

$sin^{2}\,{x}\;=\;\tfrac{1}{2}(1-cos^{2}\,{x})$ and $cos^{2}\,{x}\;=\;\tfrac{1}{2}(1+cos\,{2x})$

EXAMPLE 3 Evaluate $\int^{\pi}_{0}\,sin^{2}\,{x}\;dx$.

SOLUTION If we write $sin^{2}\,{x}\,=\,1-\,cos^{2}\,{x}$, the integral is no simpler to evaluate. Using the half-angle formula for $sin^{2}\,{x}$, however, we have

Evaluate $\int^{\pi}_{0}{sin^{2}\,{x}\;dx}\;=\;\tfrac{1}{2}\int^{\pi}{0}{(1-cos\,{2x})}\;dx$
$\qquad=[\tfrac{1}{2}(x-\tfrac{1}{2}sin\,{2x}]^{\pi}_{0}$
$=\tfrac{1}{2}(\pi\,-\,\tfrac{1}{2}sin\,{2x})-\tfrac{1}{2}(0\,\tfrac{1}{2}sin\,{0})\;=\;\tfrac{1}{2}\pi$

Notice that we mentally made the substitution u − 2x when integrating cos 2x. Another method for evaluating this integral was given in Exercise 7.1.53.

EXAMPLE 4 Find $\int\,sin^{4}\,{x}\;dx$.

SOLUTION We could evaluate this integral using the reduction formula for $\int\,sin^{n}\,{x}\;dx$ (Equation 7.1.7) together with Example 3 (as in Exercise 7.1.53), but a better method is to write $sin^{4}\,{x}\;=\;(sin^{2}\,{x})^{2}$ and use a half-angle formula:

$\int\,sin^{4}\,{x}\;dx\;=\;\int\,(sin^{2}\,{x})^{2}\;dx$
$\qquad=\int\,[\tfrac{1}{2}(1\,-\,cos\,{2x})]^{2}\;dx$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{4}\int\,[1\,-\,2\,cos\,{2x}\,+\,cos^{2}\,(2x)]\;dx$

Since $cos^{2}\,(2x)$ occurs, we use the half-angle formula for cosine to write

$cos^{2}\,({2x})\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}[1\,+\,cos(2 \cdot 2x)]\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}\,(1\,+\,cos\,{4x})$

This gives

$\int\,sin^{4}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\tfrac{1}{4}\int\,[1\,-\,2\,cos\,{2x}\,+\,\tfrac{1}{2}\,(1\,+\,cos\,{4x})]\;dx$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{4}\int(\tfrac{3}{2}\,-\,2\,cos\,{2x}\,+\,\tfrac{1}{2}\,cos\,{4x})\;dx$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{4}(\tfrac{3}{2}x\,-\,sin\,{2x}\,+\,\tfrac{1}{8}\,sin\,{4x})\,+\,C$

To summarize, we list guidelines to follow when evaluating integrals of the form $\int\,sin^{m}\,{x} \cdot cos^{n}\,{x}\;dx$, where $m `\geq`slant 0$ and $ n \geqslant 0 $ are integers.

Strategy for Evaluating $\int\,sin^{m}\,{x}\cdot cos^{n}\,{x}\;dx$

  • (a) If the power of cosine is odd $(n\,=\,2k\,+\,1)$, save one cosine factor and use $cos^{2}\,{x}\,=\,1\,-\,sin^{2}\,{x}$ to express the remaining factors in terms of sine:
  • $\int\,sin^{m}\,{x}\cdot cos^{2k+1}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\int\,sin^{m}\,{x}\,(cos^{2}\,{x})^{k}\,cos\,x\;dx$
    $\qquad=\int\,sin^{m}\,{x}\,(1\,-\,sin^{2}\,{x})^{k}\,cos\,{x}\;dx$
  • Then substitute $u\,=\,sin\,{x}$ See Example 1.
  • (b) If the power of sine is odd $(m\,=\,2k\,+\,1)$, save one sine factor and use $sin^{2}\,{x}\,=\,1\,-\,cos^{2}\,{x}$ to express the remaining factors in terms of cosine:
  • $\int\,sin^{2k+1}\,{x} \cdot cos^{n}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\int\,(sin^{2}\,{x})^{k} \cdot cos^{n}\,{x} \cdot cos^{n}\,{x} \cdot sin\,{x}\;dx$
    $\qquad=\int\,(1\,-\,cos^{2}\,{x})^{k} \cdot cos^{n}\,{x} \cdot sin\,{x}\;dx$
  • Then substitute $u\,=\,cos\,x$. See Example 2.
    [Note that if the powers of both sine and cosine are odd, either (a) or (b) can be used.]
  • (c) If the powers of both sine and cosine are even, use the half-angle identities
  • $sin^{2}\,{x}\,=\,\tfrac {1}{2}(1\,-\,cos\,{2x})$
  • $cos^{2}\,{x}\,=\,\tfrac {1}{2}(1\,+\,cos\,{2x})$
  • See Exa,ples 3 and 4.
  • It is sometimes helpful to use the identity
  • $sin\,{x} \cdot cos\,{x}\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}sin\,{2x}$

Integrals of Powers of Secant and Tangent
We use similar reasoning to evaluate integrals of the form $\int\,tan^{m}\,{x} \cdot sec^{n}\,{x}\;dx$. Because $(d/dx)\,tan\,x\,=\,sec^{2}\,x$, we can separate a $sec^{2}\,x$ factor and convert the remaining (even) power of secant to an expression involving tangent using the identity $sec^{2}\,{x}\,=\,1\,+\,tan^{2}\,x$. Or, since $(d/dx)\,sec\,{x}\,=\,sec\,{x} \cdot tan\,{x}$, we can separate a $sec\,{x} \ cdot tan\,{x}$ factor and convert the remaining (even) power of tangent to secant.

EXAMPLE 5 Evaluate $\int\,tan^{6}\,{x} \cdot sec^{4}\,{x}\;dx$.

SOLUTION If we separate one $sec^{2}\,{x}$ factor, we can express the remaining $sec^{2}\,{x}$ factor in terms of tangent using the identity $sec^{2}\,{x}\,+\,tan^{2}\,{x}$ We can then evaluate the integral by substituting $u\,=\,tan\,{x}$ so that $du\,=\,sec^{2}\,{x}\;dx$:

$\int\,tan{6}\,{x} \cdot sec^{4}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\int tan^{6}\,{x} \cdot sec^{2}\,{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\,\int tan^{6}\,{x}\,(1\,+\,tan^{2}\,{x})\,sec^{2}\,{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\,\int u^{6}\,(1\,+\,u^{2})\;du\,=\,\int (u^{6}\,+\,u^{8})\;du$
$\qquad=\,\tfrac{u^{7}}{7}\,+\,\tfrac{u^{9}}{9}\,+\,C\,=\,\tfrac{1}{7}tan^{7}\,{x}\,+\,\tfrac{1}{9}tan^{9}\,{x}\,+\,C$

EXAMPLE 6 Find $\int tan^{5}\,{\theta} \cdot sec^{7}\,{\theta}$.

SOLUTION If we separate a $sec^{2}\,{\theta}$ factor, as in the preceding example, we are left with a $sec^{5}\,{\theta}$ factor, which isn’t easily converted to tangent. However, if we separate a $sec\,{\theta} \cdot tan\,{\theta}$ factor, we can convert the remaining power of tangent to an expression involving only secant using the identity $tan^{2}\,{\theta}\,=\,sec^{2}\,{\theta}\,-\,1$. We can then evaluate the integral by substituting $u\,=\,sec\,{\theta}$, so $du\,=\,sec\,{\theta} \cdot tan\,{\theta}\;d\theta$:

$\int tan^{5}\,{\theta} \cdot sec^{7}\,{\theta}\;d\theta\,=\,\int tan^{4}\,{\theta} \cdot sec^{6}\,{\theta} \cdot sec\,{\theta} \cdot tan\,{\theta}\;d\theta$
$\qquad=\int (sec^{2}\,{\theta}\,-\,1)^{2} \cdot sec^{6}\,{\theta} \cdot sec\,{\theta} \cdot tan\,{\theta}\;d\theta$
$\qquad=\int (u^{2}\,-\,1)^{2} \cdot u^{6}\;du$
$\qquad=\int (u^{10}\,-\,2u^{8}\,+\,u^{6})\;du$
$\qquad=\tfrac{u^{11}}{11}\,-\,2\tfrac{u^{9}}{9}\,+\,\tfrac{u^{7}}{7}\,+\,C$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{11}sec^{11}\,{\theta}\,-\,\tfrac{2}{9}sec^{9}\,{\theta}\,+\,\tfrac{1}{7}sec^{7}\,{\theta}\,+\,C$

The preceding examples demonstrate strategies for evaluating integrals of the form $\int tan^{m}\,{x} \cdot sec^{n}\,{x}\;dx$ for two cases, which we summarize here.

Strategy for Evaluating $\int tan^{m}\,{x} \cdot sec^{n}\,{x}\;dx$

  • (a) If the power of secant is even $(n\,=\,2k,\,k\,\geqslant\,2)$, save a factor of $sec^{2}\,{x}$ and use $sec^{2}\,{x}\,=\,1\,+\,tan^{2}\,{x}$ to express the remaining factors in terms of $tan\,{x}$ :
  • $\int tan^{m} \cdot sec^{2k}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\int tan^{m}\,{x} \cdot (sec^{2}\,{x})^{k-1} \cdot sec^{2}\,{x}\;dx$
    $\qquad=\int tan^{m}\,{x} \cdot (1\,+\,tan^{2}\,{x})^{k-1} \cdot sec^{2}\,{x}\;dx$
  • Then substitute $u\,=\,tan\,{x}$. See Example 5.
  • (b) If the power of tangent is odd $(m\,=\,2k\,+\,1)$, save a factor of $sec\,{x} \cdot tan\,{x}$ and use $tan^{2}\,{x}\,=\,sec^{2}\,{x}\,-\,1$ to express the remaining factors in terms of $sec\,{x}$:
  • $\int tan^{2k+1}\,{x} \cdot sec^{n}\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\int (tan^{2}\,{x})^{k} \cdot sec^{n-1}\,{x} \cdot sec\,{x} \cdot tan\,{x}\;dx$
    $\qquad=\int(sec^{2}\,{x}\,-\,1)^{k} \cdot sec^{n-1}\,{x} \cdot sec\,{x} \cdot tan\,{x}\;dx$
  • Then substitute $u\,=\,sec\,{x}$. See Example 6.

For other cases, the guidelines are not as clear-cut. We may need to use identities, integration by parts, and occasionally a little ingenuity. We will sometimes need to be able to integrate $tan\,{x}$ by using the formula established in Chapter 6:

$\int tan\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\ln\,{|sec\,{x}|\,+\,C}$

We will also need the indefinite integral of secant:

$\int sec\,{x}\;dx\,=\,\ln\,{|\sec\,{x}\,+\,\tan\,{x}}|\,+\,C$

We could verify Formula 1 by differentiating the right side, or as follows. First we multiply numerator and denominator by $sec\,{x}\,+\,tan\,{x}$:

$\int \sec{x}\;dx\,=\,\int sec\,{x}\,\tfrac{sec\,{x}\,+\,tan\,{x}}{sec\,{x}\,+\,tan\,{x}}\;dx$
$\qquad=\,\int \tfrac{\sec^{2}\,{x}\,+\,\sec\,{x} \cdot \tan\,{x}}{\sec\,{x}\,+\,\tan\,{x}}\;dx$

If we substitute $u\,=\,\sec{x}\,+\,\tan{x}$, then $du\,=\,(\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x}\,+\,\sec^{2}{x})\;dx$, so the integral becomes $\int (1/u)\;du\,=\,\ln{\|\,u\,\|}\,+\,C$. Thus we have

EXAMPLE 7 Find $\int\tan^{3}{x}\;dx.$

SOLUTION Here only $\tan{x}$ occurs, so we use $\tan^{2}{x}\,=\,\sec^{2}{x}\,-\,1$ to rewrite a $\tan^{2}{x}$ factor in terms of $\sec^{2}{x}$:

$\int\tan^{3}{x}\;dx\,=\,\int\tan{x} \cdot \tan^{2} {x}\;dx\,=\,\int\tan{x} \cdot (\sec^{2}{x}\,-\,1)\;dx$
$\qquad=\int\tan{x} \cdot \sec^{2}{x}\;dx\,-\,\int\tan{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\tfrac{1}{2}\tan^{2}{x}\,-\,\ln{|\sec{x}|}\,+\,C$

In the first integral we mentally substituted $u\,=\,\tan{x}$ so that $du\,=\,\sec^{2}{x}\;dx$.

If an even power of tangent appears with an odd power of secant, it is helpful to express the integrand completely in terms of $\sec{x}$. Powers of $\sec{x}$ may require integration by parts, as shown in the following example.

EXAMPLE 8 Find $\int\sec^{3}{x}\;dx$.

SOLUTION Here we integrate by parts with

$u\,=\,\sec{x} \rightarrow du\,=\,\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x}\;dx$
$dv\,=\,\sec^{2}{x}\;dx \rightarrow b\,=\,\tan{x}$

Then

$\int\sec^{3}{x}\;dx\,=\,\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x}\,-\,\int\sec{x} \cdot \tan^{2}{x}\;dx$
$\qquad=\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x} \,-\, \int\sec{x} \cdot (\sec^{2}{x}\,-\,1)\;dx$
$\qquad=\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x}\,-\,\int\sec^{3}{x}\;dx\,+\,\int\sec{x}\;dx$

Using Formula 1 and solving for the required integral, we get

$\int\sec^{3}{x}\;dx\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}\,\sec{x} \cdot \tan{x}\,+\,\ln{|\sec{x}\,+\,\tan{x}|}\,+\,C$

Integrals such as the one in the preceding example may seem very special but they occur frequently in applications of integration, as we will see in Chapter 8. Finally, integrals of the form

$\int\cot^{m}{x} \cdot \csc^{n}{x}\;dx$

can be found in a similar way by using the identity $1\,+\,\cot^{2}{x}\,=\,\csc^{2}{x}$.

Using Product Identities

The following product identities are useful in evaluating certain trigonometric integrals.

To evaluate the integrals (a) $\int\sin{mx} \cdot \cos{n}\;dx$, (b) $\int\sin{mx} \cdot \sin{nx}\;dx$, or (c) $\int\cos{mx} \cdot \cos{nx}\;dx$, use the corresponding identity:

  • (a) $\sin{A} \cdot \cos{B}\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}[\sin{(A\,-\,B}\,+\,\sin{(A\,+\,B)}]$
  • (b) $\sin{A} \cdot \sin{B}\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}[\cos{(A\,-\,B}\,-\,\cos{(A\,+\,B)}]$
  • (c) $\cos{A} \cdot \cos{B}\,=\,\tfrac{1}{2}[\cos{(A\,-\,B}\,+\,\cos{(A\,+\,B)}]$

우리가 사용하는 여러 사물들의 모습에는 수학적인 개념이 많이 숨어있습니다. 대표적인 예시로 키호스크가 있습니다.
키호스크를 디자인 할 당시에, 사용하는 사람들을 고려하여 제작하면 접근성을 높일 수 있다는 것을 알았다.
이에 크게 두 가지의 변수을 고려하기로 하였다.

  • 눈과 디바이스의 거리
  • 눈의 생물학적 판독 영역

위 두 가지의 변수는 각각 사용자의 팔 길이, 시야각으로 해석할 수 있다.
이에 평균적인 여자아이의 팔 길이와 시야각을 측정하여 키호스크를 디자인 하였을 때, 가로 60cm 세로 33cm 정도의 영역을 구할 수 있었다.
이와 같이 수학적인 근거가 있는 디자인은 설득력과 신뢰를 확보하기에 용이하다.
